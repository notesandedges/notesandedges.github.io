5/5/17: Am I Insane?

"The definition of insanity is doing the same thing over and over and expecting different results." — Questionably Albert Einstein

I was part of a precollege classical music program during high school, and one mandatory class we had to take was jazz. I didn't know why this was, but I was excited, because jazz sounded cool.

Unfortunately, I was severely disappointed. Not by jazz itself, but by the fact that it didn't come easy to me. I like to think of myself as a resilient person, but sometimes when I reflect on my past, I am surprised by just how brittle I am.

This happened a number of times in my violin career. I'm a very ambitious person, and usually I succeed in being above–average at what I do. But when I don't, I get very discouraged. Like, my standard amount of effort didn't work, what's the point in trying anymore?

I know I shouldn't compare myself to other people, but this is often a confounding factor. I don't resent people who do better than me—that is definite. I know what it feels like to be resented for succeeding. If I start to feel resentment, I push it away because I know I'm just mad at myself.

But then I'm back to the same issue—I feel disappointed in myself and I don't try harder. That's what I mean by being "brittle". I just give up too easily. And it's funny because I already knew that about myself, I just didn't know how deep that habit was until this semester.

So why did I think I was resilient? Well, if you go through a lot of rejection, you might think you're good at handling it. It's some sort of messed up self–flattery. But really, failing a bunch of times when you're doing the same thing over and over is just insanity.

I guess that means failing twice only hurts if you didn't try harder the second time. To use the contrapositive, if you try harder every time, you won't be disappointed no matter the results (Can I pass my Concepts final now?).

Of course, sometimes you fail and it's out of your control. Just thinking about that bothers me. The first time my dad told me "just try your best in life", I said "but what if I fail?" But in practice, I find I can accept it. I applied to an REU at Cornell this summer and didn't make it in. The professor who wrote my rec letter had told me that first–years tend not to get REUs because they favor seniors.

At first I thought this meant that I needed to take more classes, but that confused me since a large part of research is learning new material anyway. I even expanded my application, describing how qualified I am at 'learning', which would make up for my lack of experience. But then I met with my prof again and she elaborated that it's because seniors simply need the opportunity more than I do. I realized that no matter what I said in my app, if this was the mindset, then if I didn't get the REU, it wasn't my fault.

This is an example of something else I learned about myself this year: I often make incorrect assumptions. Like, thinking that I can't handle failure, when I can. As long as I feel I tried, it doesn't bother me. It's like when people say, "it's the journey that counts". I've only ever believed that superficially. Like, sure, the experience of working hard is always helpful and enlightening, but how can the destination not matter? I've never believed that, but now I think I see it. It's something you find to be true. Even if you don't reach your goals, you will feel satisfied if you worked hard.

I thought I wouldn't feel satisfied, or something like that. I guess that's what people mean by "fear of failure".

I feel a bit better knowing that somehow, I'll find peace even if I fail. I don't understand why that happens, but I'm grateful to biology for making it so.
