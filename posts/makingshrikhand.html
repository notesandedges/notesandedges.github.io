Making Shrikhand..........................8/13/16




One thing my parents like to do together is make Indian sweets. It's kind of like team bonding, but for our family. Likeâ€¦family bonding. Yeah, that's it.

After some years of observation, I've decided that making Indian sweets is an activity to be contained within the privacy of your own home. This way, you're sheltered from opinionated relatives who each have something to say about the process behind making the traditional cuisine.

My parents themselves are very opinionated as well:


     Us: *return from relatives' house*
     My mom: I'd be embarrassed to serve mohanthal like that to guests.


     Us: *watching a YouTube tutorial on making shrikhand*
     My dad: He used saffron coloring. Somebody shoot him.


It's sometimes amusing to witness them bickering like an old married couple:


     Us: *four shrikhand videos later*
     My dad: She didn't put in enough elaichi (cardamom)
     My mom: Yes she did. You don't understand that you only put in a little bit for flavor. You're the kind of person who sees a little bag of elaichi, that was the harvest of an entire field back in India, and says "Dump it!" You need to chill.
     My dad: Fine. I'll make the shrikhand myself. I am chill.
     Then they went away and took naps in separate rooms.


It's also amusing how they handle the opinions of their elders. My dad is convinced the perfect Indian sweets can only be replicated from the recipes of his butter–obsessed Gujarati mother. Meanwhile, my mom told me while my grandma was visiting to wait until this week to make shrikhand, when the house would be "cleared of people."

It's not that we're very 'Indian' either. We don't got to the temple every week or wear Indian clothes more than once a year. If anything, making Indian sweets is our family's way of bonding through our ancestral roots. It also tastes good.

So without further ado, and with much hope that this post doesn't fall into a relative's Internet browser, here's our recipe for shrikhand:


Ingredients:

     2lbs/32 oz plain Greek yogurt
     3 cups powdered/ confectioner's sugar
     1/4 teaspoon crushed saffron
     1 tablespoon milk
     9 crushed cardamoms
     8 chopped unsalted pistachios


1)   Squeeze all the moisture out of the yogurt: take a muslin cloth, or any light cloth that's not a towel, scoop all of the yogurt into its center, and hang it or put weight onto it with a bowl underneath so that all of the water leaks out. This will take a couple hours and some extra weight, such as math textbooks.

  

2)   Get the color: Warm the milk and mix with the saffron and let sit in a separate bowl

3)   Assemble the shrikhand: When the yogurt is squeezed completely (you should have about 3 cups), whisk with sugar (can be sifted) one cup at a time, cardamom, and saffron/ milk mixture. To completely empty the saffron/milk mixture from its bowl, scoop a bit of the yogurt back out and move it around the bowl to soak up the color.



4)   Keep whisking until the lumps are gone and the shrikhand is smooth, then sprinkle the pistachios on top.
