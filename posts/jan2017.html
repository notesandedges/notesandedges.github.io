January 25, 2017

It's only been 20 days and 2017 is already so different than 2016.

This semester seems to be off to a shaky start. It's definitely a step up from last semester, with more new material and more work. It's now that I'm starting to realize maybe I've overcommitted myself even though I tried to avoid it. But then I realized, that's just how I am. I like being busy, and I get bored with things easily. I'm trying hard to adjust and change my habits so I can deal with all of it. A large part of that is making peace with the fact that all of this is new to me, so my usual strategies for planning, studying, working might not hold.

One thing I'm excited about is the mathematics course I'm taking. It doesn't focus on a certain field like Algebra or Calculus; it's more of an introduction to how to think mathematically and construct proofs. Right now, it's my favorite class for a number of reasons. First, the professor is pretty cool:

*Proves Pythagorean Theorem by drawing a square*
Professor: So there's a proof of the Pythagorean theorem, which I think if you don't like that you might have no soul.

Professor: You probably have already heard of this but an integer has 'even parity' if it is divisible by 2. So 14 is even because it is 7*2. And, if you haven't made peace with this yet, 0 is an even number because 0=0*2.

Professor: That box means the proof is over. We used to write Q.E.D. which means something in Latin like 'that which was to be demonstrated'. But then we decided that sounded too pretentious so now we just draw this box instead.

Second, this is the only class I have with an appreciable 'Collaboration Policy'. As in, we are allowed to collaborate on homework assignments as long as we list the people we worked with when handing in our work. One aspect of college that I was looking forward to was collaboration, and so I'm glad to finally be practicing it.

The best part of this class is the learning atmosphere that it highlights. This course has a notorious reputation at my school because it attracts students of all majors and mathematics professors of all temperaments. I was a bit terrified when I registered for it and still am waiting for the worst to happen. Part of the reason for this was because of a conversation I had with a friend who took this class last semester (with a different teacher). He told me that there are a lot of proofs and "you kind of just have to have this intuition to be able to do them. If you don't, then there's not much you can do."

I despise when people say things like this about a topic I know well because in my experience, "smartness" isn't a tangible enough phenomenon to justify us not being good enough at something. But, my friend seemed pretty confident, and I had no idea about this course, so I kind of just accepted it. Another thing he said was that this course was a "weed–out" course, meant to make all of the students who didn't have the "intuition" to drop out so that in later courses they wouldn't struggle as much. I told my friend this was a stupid way to provide students with a proper education, and he was like "Yeahâ€¦I don't know."

Fortunately, on our first day of class, my professor addressed these topics immediately. He told us that whoever may have said that the proofs in this course would "come naturally to some people is completely wrong. My goal is that you will struggle in this course, and while struggle generally has a negative connotation, in this course it will help you. So don't misunderstand—this is not a weed–out course."

Many classmates whom I spoke with after our first lecture said our professor's words made them feel more confident, which is nice.

In other news, I think I'm making headway as a member of the Hyperloop team, but in a roundabout way. For some reason, even though I joined the team back in September, the issue of me not being a masters student and thus lacking significant knowledge of mechanical engineering was never brought up. I realized this when talking to a new member the other day and he told me to review a bunch of concepts about the "Strength of Materials" and then learn Solidworks. At first I was a bit angry. I was constantly frustrated last semester that I couldn't seem to contribute much to the team, so when the gaps in my education were directly addressed (and I was given an actual course of action), I felt annoyed that no one had brought it up sooner.

Now though, I realize I may have been getting ahead of myself. The structure of the SpaceX competition is such that much of the design phase for this year's round was completed last year, so there really wasn't much I could do. I joined when all of the finishing touches were being made, and so all I could really help with was manual labor.

Now that classes are getting harder and I've had this realization about Hyperloop, I have to remember not to blame myself if things go wrong. Or, I have to remember to forgive myself.
